Understanding AQI

Everything you need to know about Air Quality Index

What is AQI?

The Air Quality Index (AQI) is a standardized indicator developed to communicate how polluted the air currently is or how polluted it is forecast to become. It transforms complex air quality data into a single number, color code, and descriptive category that people can easily understand.

In India, the National Air Quality Index (NAQI) was launched by the Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change in October 2014. It considers eight pollutants: PM10, PM2.5, NO₂, SO₂, CO, O₃, NH₃, and Pb. The AQI value is the maximum of sub-indices computed for each pollutant.

How is AQI Calculated?

AQI is calculated using the following steps:

  • Step 1: Measure the concentration of each pollutant (PM2.5, PM10, NO₂, SO₂, CO, O₃) over a specified averaging period.
  • Step 2: Calculate a sub-index for each pollutant using breakpoint concentration tables defined by CPCB (Central Pollution Control Board).
  • Step 3: The overall AQI is the maximum of all sub-indices. The pollutant with the highest sub-index is called the dominant pollutant.

Sub-Index Formula

For each pollutant, the sub-index (I) is calculated using linear interpolation:

I = ((I_high - I_low) / (C_high - C_low)) × (C - C_low) + I_low

Where C is the pollutant concentration, and breakpoints (C_low, C_high, I_low, I_high) are from CPCB standards.

AQI Categories & Health Impact

AQI Range Category Health Impact Advice
0 – 50 Good Minimal impact on health Enjoy outdoor activities freely
51 – 100 Satisfactory Minor breathing discomfort to sensitive people Sensitive individuals should limit prolonged outdoor exertion
101 – 200 Moderate Breathing discomfort to people with lung/heart disease Reduce prolonged outdoor exertion. Use masks if sensitive
201 – 300 Poor Breathing discomfort on prolonged exposure Avoid outdoor activities. Use N95 masks. Keep windows closed
301 – 400 Very Poor Respiratory illness on prolonged exposure Stay indoors. Use air purifiers. Avoid all outdoor exercise
401 – 500 Severe Serious health effects for everyone Stay indoors, seal windows. Medical emergency preparedness

Key Pollutants Explained

PM2.5

Fine Particulate Matter — Particles smaller than 2.5 micrometers. They penetrate deep into lungs and bloodstream. Major sources: vehicle exhaust, industrial emissions, crop burning. Most dangerous air pollutant for human health.

PM10

Coarse Particulate Matter — Particles smaller than 10 micrometers. Sources include dust, pollen, construction. Can cause respiratory issues but less dangerous than PM2.5 as they don't penetrate as deep.

NO₂ (Nitrogen Dioxide)

Reddish-brown gas from vehicle emissions and power plants. Irritates airways and increases susceptibility to respiratory infections. Contributes to smog and acid rain formation.

SO₂ (Sulfur Dioxide)

Produced by burning fossil fuels (coal, oil) in power plants and industrial facilities. Causes respiratory problems and contributes to acid rain. Reacts in atmosphere to form fine particles.

CO (Carbon Monoxide)

Colorless, odorless gas from incomplete combustion. Vehicle exhaust is the primary source. Reduces oxygen delivery in blood. High concentrations can be fatal in enclosed spaces.

O₃ (Ozone)

Ground-level ozone is formed when NOx and VOCs react in sunlight. Unlike stratospheric ozone (which protects us), ground-level ozone triggers chest pain, coughing, and asthma.

Delhi's Air Quality Challenge

Delhi consistently ranks among the world's most polluted cities. Key factors contributing to poor air quality include:

  • Vehicular Emissions: Over 12 million registered vehicles contribute ~40% of PM2.5 in Delhi.
  • Crop Burning: Stubble burning in Punjab and Haryana during Oct-Nov causes severe spikes in AQI.
  • Industrial Emissions: Factories in NCR and surrounding areas release harmful pollutants.
  • Construction Dust: Rapid urbanization and construction generates significant PM10.
  • Geography: Delhi's landlocked location traps pollutants, especially during winter inversions.
  • Winter Inversion: Cold air traps pollutants near ground level, causing AQI to spike above 400+ during November–January.
